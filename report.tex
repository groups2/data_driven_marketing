\begin{abstract}
Modern e-commerce platforms generate large-scale clickstream
data characterized by sparsity, behavioral heterogeneity,
and highly imbalanced transaction distributions. Traditional
revenue prediction models often struggle to capture customer
purchasing behavior when only a small proportion of sessions
generate revenue. This study proposes a business-oriented
machine learning framework for conversion and revenue
prediction using the Google Merchandise Store dataset from
Google Analytics.

The proposed pipeline integrates behavioral feature
engineering techniques, including Recency-Frequency-Monetary
(RFM) metrics, session velocity indicators, and temporal
browsing patterns. To address the zero-inflated nature of
transaction revenue, the framework employs a two-stage
XGBoost hurdle architecture consisting of a calibrated
classifier for purchase probability estimation and a
weighted regressor for conditional revenue prediction.

Beyond conventional statistical evaluation, the framework
incorporates business-oriented metrics such as Revenue
Capture Curves and Lift analysis, while connecting
predictive outputs with A/B testing strategies for campaign
optimization and customer targeting. Experimental results
show that behavioral engagement, acquisition channels, and
temporal interaction patterns provide strong predictive
signals for both conversion probability and revenue
generation. The proposed framework offers an interpretable
and scalable solution for intelligent e-commerce analytics.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
E-commerce analytics, clickstream analysis, XGBoost, hurdle models, customer conversion prediction, revenue forecasting, feature engineering, behavioral analytics.

\section{Introduction}
The rapid growth of e-commerce platforms has transformed
customer interactions into large-scale behavioral datasets.
Every browsing session, product interaction, and marketing
touchpoint generates clickstream information that may reveal
purchasing intent and customer value. However, extracting
meaningful insights from such data remains challenging due
to semi-structured formats, noisy browsing behavior, and
extreme transaction imbalance.

The Google Merchandise Store dataset provides a realistic
analytics environment collected from Google Analytics,
containing session-level information related to devices,
traffic sources, geographic attributes, and transaction
activities. A major characteristic of the dataset is that
only a very small proportion of sessions generate revenue,
while the majority correspond to non-purchasing browsing
behavior. This imbalance limits the effectiveness of
conventional single-stage predictive approaches for purchase
and revenue modeling.

To address these challenges, this study proposes an
integrated analytical framework consisting of four major
components. First, advanced feature engineering techniques
are applied to extract behavioral signals from clickstream
data, including RFM indicators, session velocity,
engagement depth, and temporal browsing patterns. Second,
a two-stage hurdle architecture based on XGBoost is
introduced to separately model purchase probability and
conditional transaction value through a calibrated
classifier and weighted regressor. Third, the framework
adopts business-oriented evaluation metrics such as Revenue
Capture Curves and Lift analysis to assess practical
commercial performance beyond conventional statistical
accuracy measures. Finally, predictive modeling outputs are
connected with causal validation through A/B testing
strategies designed to support campaign optimization and
customer targeting decisions.

The objective of this research is to develop an
interpretable and scalable framework capable of converting
raw behavioral analytics data into actionable business
insights for conversion prediction and revenue optimization.
\section{About Data}

This study uses the Google Merchandise Store dataset, a large-scale e-commerce clickstream dataset exported from Google Analytics. The data describe visits to the Google Merchandise Store, an online retail platform selling Google-branded products. Each record corresponds to one website session and is associated with a unique visitor identifier, allowing repeated visits by the same user to be observed over time. The original dataset contains 1,708,337 session records from 1,323,730 unique users, covering user activity from July 2016 to May 2018.

The dataset is particularly suitable for data-driven marketing analysis because it captures the full digital customer journey from acquisition to on-site engagement and, for a small subset of users, transaction generation. The primary predictive target is \textit{totals\_transactionRevenue}, which records purchase revenue at the session level. As is common in real-world e-commerce data, the target variable is highly zero-inflated: only 18,514 sessions, or approximately 1.08\% of all sessions, contain positive revenue before cleaning. At the user level, the post-cleaning data contain 1,272,948 unique visitors, of whom only 14,303 users generated revenue, corresponding to a buyer rate of 1.12\%. This imbalance makes the dataset appropriate for studying both conversion propensity and conditional customer value.

The raw data combine conventional tabular identifiers with semi-structured Google Analytics fields. Core session identifiers include \textit{fullVisitorId}, \textit{visitId}, \textit{visitNumber}, \textit{visitStartTime}, and \textit{date}. In addition, several columns are stored as nested JSON objects rather than directly usable tabular variables. The most important JSON groups are \textit{device}, \textit{geoNetwork}, \textit{totals}, and \textit{trafficSource}. After parsing, these fields provide five main categories of information:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Session identification variables, which distinguish users, visits, visit order, and time of access.
    \item Device variables, including browser, operating system, mobile indicator, and device category.
    \item Geographic variables, including continent, sub-continent, country, region, metro area, city, and network domain.
    \item Behavioral engagement variables, including hits, pageviews, bounces, session quality, time on site, transactions, and revenue.
    \item Acquisition variables, including channel grouping, traffic source, medium, campaign, keyword, referral path, and advertising metadata.
\end{itemize}

Exploratory inspection also showed that the dataset contains strong behavioral heterogeneity. Most visitors interact with only a small number of pages, whereas a limited group of highly engaged users generate many hits and pageviews. Revenue is similarly concentrated: among purchasing users, the top 1\% of buyers account for 28.0\% of revenue, while the top 20\% account for 75.3\%. These patterns motivate a modeling design that separates the probability of purchase from the magnitude of revenue conditional on purchase.

\section{Methodology}

%--------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Data preprocessing \& understanding}

The preprocessing stage was designed to convert the raw Google Analytics export into a clean analytical table while preserving the behavioral meaning of the original tracking data. Because the dataset contains nested JSON columns, the first step was structural normalization. The fields \textit{device}, \textit{geoNetwork}, \textit{totals}, and \textit{trafficSource} were parsed using a safe JSON parser and flattened with \texttt{json\_normalize}. Each nested attribute was renamed with its parent prefix, such as \textit{device\_browser}, \textit{geoNetwork\_country}, \textit{totals\_pageviews}, and \textit{trafficSource\_medium}. This procedure transformed the semi-structured logs into a session-level feature matrix suitable for exploratory analysis and machine learning.

Two additional semi-structured fields were handled separately. The \textit{customDimensions} column was parsed to extract the first available dimension value when present, while the \textit{hits} column was summarized into a count variable representing the number of recorded hit objects in each session. Since the raw dataset is large, the parsing pipeline was implemented in chunks of 50,000 rows to reduce memory pressure and improve reproducibility.

After flattening, the preprocessing pipeline standardized invalid or non-informative placeholders. Values such as \textit{unknown.unknown}, \textit{(not set)}, \textit{not available in demo dataset}, \textit{(not provided)}, and \textit{(none)} were treated as missing values. Numeric session fields, including \textit{visitNumber}, \textit{totals\_hits}, \textit{totals\_pageviews}, \textit{totals\_transactionRevenue}, \textit{totals\_timeOnSite}, \textit{totals\_bounces}, \textit{totals\_sessionQualityDim}, and \textit{totals\_transactions}, were converted to numeric format and missing numeric entries were set to zero where absence represents no recorded activity. The \textit{date} field was converted from the original \textit{YYYYMMDD} format into a proper date variable.

Revenue required special treatment because Google Analytics exports monetary values in micro-units. Therefore, \textit{totals\_transactionRevenue} was divided by $10^{6}$ when values indicated micro-unit encoding. A logarithmic target variable was then created as

\begin{equation}
\textit{target\_log\_revenue}=\log(1+\textit{totals\_transactionRevenue}),
\end{equation}

which reduces the effect of extreme transaction values. In the exploratory analysis, this transformation reduced revenue skewness from 24.89 to 0.38, making the distribution more stable for downstream modeling.

The next step was a data-quality audit intended to remove observations that do not represent genuine customer behavior. Internal traffic was identified using network domains containing \texttt{google} and sessions from Mountain View, which are likely to include testing or employee activity. Bot-like behavior was detected using abnormal engagement patterns, including more than 500 hits with zero time on site, more than 500 pageviews with a bounce flag, and browser strings associated with automated traffic. The pipeline also checked for tracking errors, such as bounced sessions with positive revenue, and unrealistic ``fake whale'' sessions with revenue but no meaningful engagement. This cleaning step reduced the dataset from 1,708,337 to 1,623,718 sessions.

Missing values were not treated as purely random noise because, in digital analytics, missingness often reflects the acquisition context. For example, direct sessions commonly lack campaign metadata because the user did not arrive through a tracked campaign link. Accordingly, missing values were filled according to feature semantics: campaign values were replaced with \textit{no\_campaign}, traffic medium with \textit{none}, and high-missing geographic fields such as metro, city, and region with \textit{unknown}. Lower-missing categorical variables, including operating system, country, sub-continent, continent, source, and browser, were also filled with \textit{unknown}.

Several variables were removed because they were either too sparse, too granular, or redundant for generalizable modeling. The dropped columns include \textit{trafficSource\_adwordsClickInfo.gclId}, \textit{geoNetwork\_networkDomain}, \textit{trafficSource\_keyword}, \textit{etc}. Specifically, variables related to advertising metadata were dominated by missing or placeholder values, while \textit{geoNetwork\_networkDomain} and \textit{trafficSource\_referralPath} were highly granular identifiers with limited generalizable signal. After this step, the preprocessed dataset contained 53 columns.

Next, acquisition and temporal variables were standardized to improve interpretability. Traffic sources were grouped using regular-expression rules into broader categories such as Google, YouTube, Yahoo, Facebook, Bing, Reddit, Pinterest, Baidu, and direct traffic. Additional calendar features were derived from the session date, including month, year, and day of week. These transformations produced a cleaned session-level dataset that retains the main behavioral, geographic, device, revenue, and acquisition signals required for customer segmentation and revenue prediction.

Finally, the \textit{hits} field required additional processing because it contains the most detailed behavioral information in the Google Analytics dataset. Unlike the main session-level columns, \textit{hits} is stored as a list of nested event objects, where each object may contain page paths, hit types, product information, and e-commerce actions. Therefore, this column was not simply flattened in the same way as other JSON fields. Instead, it was parsed separately to reconstruct the user's within-session journey. From this process, several sequential features were extracted, such as \textit{page\_path\_sequence}, \textit{action\_type\_sequence}. These variables preserve the order of user actions and make it possible to analyze how customers move from browsing to product interaction and purchase behavior.

Based on the extracted hit sequences, additional funnel indicators were created to summarize key behavioral milestones in each session. These include whether a session contained a product view, add-to-cart action, checkout action, purchase action, cart page visit, or checkout page visit. The pipeline also derived abandonment-related variables such as \textit{cart\_abandonment} and \textit{checkout\_abandonment}, which identify sessions where users reached an important funnel stage but did not complete the next step. This transformation is important because it converts the raw \textit{hits} column from a complex nested object into interpretable session-level signals. As a result, the processed dataset can support not only revenue prediction and segmentation, but also funnel diagnosis and campaign design, especially for identifying where users drop off before conversion.
\subsection{Historical Data Customer Segmentation}

The customer segmentation framework was developed to transform longitudinal behavioral data into discrete, actionable cohorts. Unlike traditional segmentation models that apply a single set of rules to the entire population, this methodology utilizes a dual-pathway approach. The user base is first partitioned into two primary groups based on historical conversion: \textit{Buyers}, defined by a transaction count greater than zero, and \textit{Non-buyers}, representing visitors who have engaged with the platform but not yet converted. This bifurcation ensures that the clustering algorithm identifies patterns within homogeneous populations, preventing transaction-heavy features from masking subtle engagement signals in the non-converting majority.

\subsubsection{Feature Selection and Behavioral Scoring}

To capture the multidimensional nature of user behavior, a specialized feature set was engineered for each pathway. For the Buyer group, the segmentation utilizes eight behavioral markers: Recency, Frequency, Monetary value, Loyalty (relationship lifetime), Engagement, Bounce rate, Funnel depth, and a purchase-based Hibernation score. The Non-buyer group is evaluated using a refined subset of six features, focusing strictly on engagement, navigation patterns, and site-level activity.


The detailed K-Means feature definitions and scoring directions are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:kmeans_features}.

To ensure all features reside on a comparable scale while maintaining business interpretability, a \textit{base-aware scoring} mechanism was implemented. This method evaluates user performance relative to a defined baseline, rather than using raw values or simple min-max scaling. The scoring pipeline consists of four distinct phases:

\begin{itemize}

\item Directional Assignment: Each feature is assigned
a business directionality. Features such as Monetary and
Frequency are categorized as \textit{higher-is-better},
while Recency and Bounce Rate are classified as
\textit{lower-is-better}. A special category,
\textit{positive-lower-is-better}, is reserved for
gap-based metrics such as inter-purchase time.

\item Distance-to-Base Calculation: A baseline ($b$)
is established for each feature, representing the minimum
expected level of activity (e.g., a baseline of 1 session
or 0 days of lifetime). The distance ($d$) for a user value
($x$) is calculated as:

\begin{equation}
d =
\begin{cases}
\max(x - b, 0)
& \text{for higher-better features} \\

\max(b - x, 0)
& \text{for lower-better features}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

For gap-based metrics, the distance is calculated as the
inverse of the value ($1/x$) when $x > 0$, ensuring that
smaller gaps produce larger distances.

\item Percentile Normalization: Raw distances are
converted into percentile ranks ($p$) within each respective
group ($p \in [0,1]$). This non-linear transformation
reduces the impact of outliers and ensures that the final
scores reflect a user's relative standing within the
population.

\item Integer Mapping: Percentiles are mapped to a
discrete 1--5 scale using the floor function
$\lfloor p \times 5 \rfloor + 1$. The resulting score acts
as an intuitive "star-rating" system, where 1 represents
baseline-level performance and 5 corresponds to the top
20\% of the group.

\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Clustering Implementation and Optimization}

The K-Means algorithm was selected as the primary clustering engine due to its efficiency with high-dimensional behavioral data. Prior to clustering, the 1--5 feature scores were standardized to a mean of zero and unit variance to prevent any single dimension from dominating the Euclidean distance calculations.

The determination of the optimal number of clusters ($k$) was treated as a model selection problem, utilizing the Elbow Method in conjunction with three secondary validation indices. The Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS), also known as inertia, was plotted across a range of $k$ values to identify the "elbow point" where the marginal gain in information significantly diminishes. To ensure the robustness of the selected $k$, the following metrics were evaluated:
\begin{itemize}
\item Silhouette Coefficient: Measuring how similar an observation is to its own cluster compared to others, with a goal of maximizing this value.
\item Davies-Bouldin Index: Assessing the average similarity between each cluster and its most similar one; lower values indicate better separation.
\item Calinski-Harabasz Score: Evaluating the ratio of the sum of between-clusters scatter to within-cluster scatter.
\end{itemize}

By triangulating these indices, the methodology ensures that the resulting segments are not only statistically distinct but also possess enough density to be actionable for downstream marketing operations, such as personalized remarketing or retention modeling.

\subsection{Training Customer Revenue Prediction \& Segmentation}
\subsubsection{Data Splitting Strategy}

To ensure temporal integrity throughout training and evaluation,
the dataset is partitioned using a sliding-window cross-validation
scheme rather than a random split. The following subsections describe
the rationale, construction, and role of each fold.

The data is partitioned chronologically rather than randomly to
prevent temporal data leakage. User behavior shifts according to
seasons, promotions, and trends. A random split would allow the
model to train on December data while validating on June data --
an unrealistic scenario that inflates evaluation metrics and does
not reflect real-world deployment.

A sliding-window strategy is applied across the full labeled period
(2016-08-01 to 2018-04-30), producing 7 folds with the following
parameters: a 168-day feature window, a 92-day
target window, and a 60-day slide between consecutive
folds. For each fold, one observation per visitor is created by
aggregating session behavior within the feature window; the label
(\textit{target\_revenue}) is the total revenue generated within
the subsequent target window. Purchases within the feature window
are used only as behavioral features -- never as labels -- to
prevent leakage.

Because consecutive feature windows overlap by 108 days
(168 $-$ 60), the same visitor may appear across multiple folds
with different features and different labels.
This overlap is intentional: it increases the number of training
observations without requiring additional data, while still
preserving temporal ordering within each fold.
The resulting dataset is at the observation level, not
the user level.

\subsubsection{Feature Engineering}

After completing the exploratory analysis stage, the next objective
was to transform raw session-level clickstream data into
customer-level behavioral representations suitable for downstream
machine learning models. To avoid temporal leakage, all features
were constructed exclusively from sessions within the historical
feature window of each fold.

After completing the exploratory analysis stage, the next
objective was to transform raw session-level clickstream
data into customer-level behavioral representations suitable
for downstream machine learning models. To avoid temporal
leakage, all features were constructed exclusively from
sessions within the historical feature window of each fold.

\begin{itemize}

\item Categorical Standardization and Target Encoding: High-cardinality categorical variables -- including geographic
attributes, traffic sources, browser information, and campaign
identifiers -- were first standardized via Top-$N$ grouping,
where the optimal $N$ per column was selected automatically
using elbow detection on marginal Gini impurity reduction.
Infrequent categories were collapsed into a shared
\textit{Other} class to reduce sparsity. Target encoding was
then applied at the session level, replacing each category
with the mean transaction revenue observed in training
sessions.

\item Customer-Level Aggregation Features: Session-level interactions were aggregated by
\textit{fullVisitorId} using sum, mean, max, and count
operators over engagement signals and transaction variables
(such as \textit{totals\_hits},
\textit{totals\_pageviews},
\textit{totals\_timeOnSite},
\textit{totals\_transactions},
\textit{totals\_transactionRevenue}).
Target-encoded categorical variables were additionally
aggregated using mean, max, and last to capture both average
and most recent behavioral context. The mean hits per session
was retained as \textit{funnel\_depth}, serving as a proxy
for browsing depth within individual visits.

\item RFM, Lifetime, and Hibernation Features: Recency-Frequency-Monetary features were constructed from
historical session data, measuring temporal distance since
last visit, repeated interaction frequency, and aggregated
revenue statistics. A \textit{hibernation\_flag} was derived
by comparing each customer's recency against a data-driven
threshold, flagging customers at risk of disengagement.

Beyond standard RFM metrics, probabilistic lifetime features
were generated using the BG/NBD and Gamma-Gamma models,
producing \textit{p\_alive},
\textit{expected\_purchases\_92d}, and
\textit{expected\_monetary} as forward-looking behavioral
estimates.

\item Behavioral Diversity, Velocity, and Temporal Features: To capture cross-channel diversity, the number of unique
values per customer was computed across seven dimensions --
city, country, traffic source, medium, channel, browser, and
operating system -- and summarized into a composite
\textit{diversity\_score}. Velocity-based indicators
(\textit{vel\_pageviews},
\textit{vel\_hits},
\textit{vel\_sessions})
were constructed by comparing activity in the last 30 days
against the preceding 90-day period, capturing changes in
engagement intensity over time.

Finally, temporal features
and interaction terms were included to model cyclical browsing preferences and
non-linear engagement relationships.

\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.5em}
\subsubsection{Customer Revenue Prediction Model}

The core task is to predict the future revenue each visitor will
generate, using only their past behavioral signals. Across the full
labeled dataset, 1,377 of 1,043,941 unique visitors made at least
one purchase -- a buy rate of \text{0.13\%}. This extreme class
imbalance is the defining challenge of the modeling problem.

A traditional single-stage regression model is ineffective in this
setting: because 99.87\% of revenue values are zero, such a model
is dominated by the zero-revenue mass and learns to predict near-zero
for everyone, destroying the revenue signal from the minority of
actual buyers. The problem is therefore formulated as a
\text{two-stage process}:

\begin{itemize}

\item Who will buy? -- A classifier estimates the
probability that a visitor will make a purchase
(\textit{p\_buy}).

\item If they buy, how much? -- A regressor, trained
exclusively on buyers, estimates the expected log-revenue
(\textit{e\_revenue}).

\end{itemize}

The outputs from both stages are combined to form a composite ranking score:
\begin{equation}
\mathit{final\_score} = \mathit{p\_buy} \times \mathit{e\_revenue}
\end{equation}

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\text{a. Stage 1 -- Classification}
\vspace{0.3em}

An XGBoost Classifier is trained to estimate $p\_buy$ for each
visitor. To handle the severe class imbalance,
\textit{scale\_pos\_weight} is set to
$\sqrt{N_{\text{neg}} / N_{\text{pos}}}$
so that the minority class is upweighted without fully inverting
the class distribution. After each seed, Isotonic
Regression is fit on Validation set predictions to calibrate
output probabilities against the true observed buyer rate.
The model is evaluated using PR-AUC, optimized via early stopping
on the Validation set across 5 random seeds.

The true ensemble PR-AUC of \text{0.0894} represents a
120 times improvement over the random-guess baseline (0.000744),
with a calibration ratio of 1.01 confirming that predicted
$p\_buy$ closely matches the true buyer rate.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\text{b. Stage 2 -- Regression}
\vspace{0.3em}

An XGBoost Regressor is trained exclusively on the 1,465 buyer
observations in the Train set. The target is transformed to
$\log(1 + \text{revenue})$ to reduce right-skew and stabilize
gradient updates. Predictions are clipped at the 99th percentile
of log-revenue ($p_{99} = 21.58$) to prevent extreme values from
dominating the composite score.

Both stages are run across 5 random seeds. For each seed, the model
is first fit on the Train set with Validation-based early stopping,
then refit on the full labeled data (Folds 1--7) using best iteration before predicting on the evaluation set.
Final outputs are unweighted averages across all 5 seeds.

The regression stage reduces RMSE from the naive baseline of 18.02
down to \text{1.1961} -- a 93\% reduction -- confirming
meaningful revenue signal despite training on only 1,465
observations. The $R^2$ of 0.166, while modest, is expected: revenue
is inherently noisy and driven by rare high-value transactions; the
primary objective of this stage is to rank buyers by relative
spending, not to achieve precise point predictions.

\noindent\text{c. Customer Segmentation via K-Means}
\vspace{0.3em}

The predicted \textit{p\_buy} and \textit{e\_revenue} scores are used as two
independent axes for K-Means clustering, applied to the 119,207
evaluation-set users with $\textit{final\_score} > 0$. Both
features are standardized prior to
clustering to prevent the higher-magnitude \textit{e\_revenue} values
from dominating the distance metric.

The optimal number of clusters is selected by evaluating $k \in
\{2, \ldots, 10\}$ using Silhouette score, Davies-Bouldin index,
and Calinski-Harabasz score on a stratified sample of 30,000
observations. $k = 2$ yields the clearest separation (Silhouette
= 0.397) while remaining interpretable for marketing purposes,
and is selected as the final configuration.

\subsection{Strategy Marketing Framework}
The strategy section operationalizes three interconnected analytical workstreams. The historical RFM-based segmentation establishes behavioral archetypes across the existing customer base, informing customer relationship management priorities. A funnel drop-off analysis then identifies the highest-friction transition point within the conversion path, motivating a site-wide UX intervention evaluated through a controlled A/B experiment. Finally, a predictive CLV model re-partitions the active user base according to forward-looking purchase probability and expected revenue contribution, enabling targeted campaign allocation toward users with the greatest economic potential. While the funnel experiment optimizes the platform experience broadly, the predictive campaign targets specific high-value users with personalized conversion strategies — the two levers operating at complementary levels of the marketing stack.
\subsubsection{Funnel Strategy for Highest Drop-off Point}

\label{sec:funnel_strategy_highest_dropoff}
The final stage of the proposed framework involves translating predictive insights into a controlled marketing experiment. This phase focuses on the strategic optimization of the conversion funnel, specifically targeting the transition from product viewing to cart addition.

The treatment variant of the experiment is designed with a multi-layered approach to address psychological friction and enhance user intent. The optimization techniques are structured around three core pillars:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Information Architecture and Visual Hierarchy: This technique focuses on improving product discovery by optimizing the presentation of core product attributes. It involves the refinement of imagery, product titles, and the strategic placement of value propositions to reduce cognitive load during the browsing process.
    \item Trust and Social Proof Integration: To reduce perceived risk, the interface incorporates qualitative validation signals. This includes the prominent display of customer ratings, verified reviews, and trust badges, providing the external validation necessary to move a user from interest to intent.
    \item Urgency and Scarcity Modeling: Psychological triggers such as real-time inventory alerts (e.g., "limited stock") and countdown timers for time-sensitive promotional offers are utilized to leverage loss aversion. These elements are designed to provide a gentle nudge towards immediate action within the session.
\end{itemize}

To ensure the observed impact is causal rather than coincidental, the framework employs a randomized A/B testing design. The methodology follows a rigorous statistical protocol:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Primary Metric: The success of the intervention is measured by the \textit{Add-to-Cart (ATC) Rate}, defined as the proportion of sessions where at least one "Add to Cart" action is recorded out of the total sessions that viewed a product page. This binary metric is selected for its high sensitivity to PDP modifications and its position as a leading indicator of final conversion.
    \item Secondary Metrics: To explain the mechanism behind shifts in the primary metric, a suite of behavioral indicators is monitored. These include \textit{Average Time-on-Product-Page} to assess content engagement and \textit{Trust Signal Interaction Rate} (e.g., clicks on reviews or warranty badges). These metrics provide the necessary insights to confirm whether the observed lift is due to the specific optimizations or external noise.
    \item Guardrail Metrics: To ensure that the optimization of one funnel stage does not degrade the overall system, \textit{Guardrail Metrics} are enforced as absolute constraints. These include \textit{Page Load Latency} (to monitor the performance impact of high-fidelity media) and \textit{Checkout Success Rate}. Any statistically significant regression in these indicators triggers an immediate review or termination of the experiment to protect the core user experience.
\end{itemize}

To ensure the experiment is sufficiently powered to detect meaningful changes while controlling for false positives, a rigorous sample size calculation is performed. Given that the ATC Rate is a binary proportion, the methodology utilizes a \textit{two-proportion Z-test} for power analysis.

The required sample size ($n$) per variant is calculated using the following objective function:
\begin{equation}
n = \frac{(Z_{\alpha/2} + Z_{\beta})^2 \times [p_1(1-p_1) + p_2(1-p_2)]}{(p_2 - p_1)^2}
\end{equation}

Where the technical parameters are defined as:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $p_1$: The baseline ATC rate, established from a 168-day historical feature window.
    \item $p_2$: The target conversion rate, calculated as $p_1 \times (1 + MDE)$, where $MDE$ (Minimum Detectable Effect) is the smallest uplift that justifies the implementation cost.
    \item $Z_{\alpha/2}$: The critical value for the significance level ($\alpha = 0.05$), set at 1.96 to control the probability of Type I errors (False Positives).
    \item $Z_{\beta}$: The value for the desired statistical power ($1-\beta = 0.80$), set at 0.84 to ensure an 80\% probability of detecting a true effect.
\end{itemize}

The duration of the experiment ($D$) is not determined solely by the sample size, but also by temporal constraints to account for periodic behavioral fluctuations. The minimum duration is derived as $D = n / \textit{daily\_traffic}$. 

To mitigate the \textit{Day-of-Week Effect}—the phenomenon where user behavior varies significantly between weekdays and weekends—the testing period is strictly enforced as a multiple of 7 days (e.g., 14, 21, or 28 days). This ensures that the experiment captures a complete business cycle, thereby neutralizing time-based noise and increasing the reliability of the findings. \\
Beyond statistical significance, the campaign must demonstrate business viability. The framework incorporates a financial model to estimate the potential Return on Marketing Investment (ROMI) before full-scale deployment.

The \textit{Incremental Profit} is modeled by estimating the downstream impact of the ATC rate uplift on finalized transactions:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\textit{Incremental Profit} ={}& \Delta \textit{ATC Rate} \times \textit{Sessions} \\
& \times \textit{Purchase Rate} \times \textit{AOV} \\
& \times \textit{Gross Margin} - \textit{Project Cost}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The ultimate campaign efficiency is evaluated through the ROMI metric, which weighs the generated incremental profit against the total project expenditure:
\begin{equation}
\textit{ROMI} = \frac{\textit{Incremental Profit}}{\textit{Project Cost}}
\end{equation}

This technical approach ensures that marketing decisions are driven by both statistical rigor and financial accountability, prioritizing campaigns that offer the highest probability of positive economic impact.

\subsubsection{Segment-Level Campaign Profitability Optimization}

Building on the funnel experiment and historical segmentation outputs, an additional decision-optimization layer is introduced to determine which customer segments should receive marketing investment before any campaign is scaled. The objective of this module is not only to rank segments by predicted purchase probability, but also to evaluate whether the expected economic return of targeting each segment exceeds its acquisition and campaign cost. This creates a bridge between predictive modeling and managerial budget allocation.

The input to this stage is the final customer segmentation table, where each user is associated with a final cluster name, buyer flag, historical revenue, transaction count, and model-based purchase probability. When the machine-learning prediction score is unavailable, the framework assigns segment-specific simulated probabilities based on behavioral intent. High-Value buyers receive the highest expected probability, followed by Recent buyers, Hot Prospects, Warm Prospects, Dormant users, and low-intent groups. This fallback procedure allows the decision framework to remain operational even when production scoring is incomplete, while still preserving the behavioral hierarchy identified in segmentation.

Because Google Analytics revenue may appear either in standard currency or micro-unit format, the pipeline first validates the revenue scale by calculating the median customer-level average order value among purchasing users. If the median value is unrealistically large, revenue is converted from micro-units by dividing by $10^{6}$. Segment-level summaries are then constructed by aggregating total revenue, total transactions, average predicted purchase probability, and segment size. The observed average order value is calculated as

\begin{equation}
AOV_s = \frac{\textit{Revenue}_s}{\textit{Transactions}_s},
\end{equation}

where $s$ denotes a customer segment. For non-buyer segments with no observed transaction history, expected AOV is imputed as a proportion of the buyer benchmark AOV. Hot Prospects are assigned 95\% of buyer AOV, Warm Prospects 80\%, Dormant users 75\%, and lower-intent groups 55\%. This imputation reflects the assumption that higher-intent non-buyers are closer to buyer-like value than broad low-intent visitors.

The framework then assigns segment-specific unit-economics parameters. Gross margin and customer acquisition cost are varied by segment to reflect different campaign costs and expected profitability. For example, High-Value customers are assigned relatively high margin and low acquisition cost, while General and Low-Intent users receive lower margins and higher CAC assumptions. To account for operational uncertainty, CAC is simulated stochastically as

\begin{equation}
CAC_s^{sim} \sim \mathcal{N}(CAC_s, (0.15 \times CAC_s)^2),
\end{equation}

with simulated values clipped at a minimum of one dollar. This converts the decision model from a deterministic ranking exercise into a more realistic profitability simulation.

For each segment, the minimum break-even conversion rate is calculated as

\begin{equation}
CR_{min,s}=\frac{CAC_s^{sim}}{AOV_s \times GM_s},
\end{equation}

where $GM_s$ is the segment-specific gross margin. Expected campaign profit is then estimated as

\begin{equation}
\textit{Profit}_s = N_s \times CR_s \times AOV_s \times GM_s - \textit{Spend}_s,
\end{equation}

where $N_s$ is segment size, $CR_s$ is the model-implied purchase probability, and $\textit{Spend}_s=N_s \times CAC_s^{sim}$. Campaign efficiency is measured through ROMI:

\begin{equation}
ROMI_s = \frac{\textit{Profit}_s}{\textit{Spend}_s}.
\end{equation}

The final prioritization rule combines statistical propensity with economic feasibility. A segment is classified as \textit{Scale Aggressively} when ROMI is greater than one and the predicted conversion rate exceeds the break-even threshold. It is classified as \textit{Scale Carefully} when ROMI is positive and conversion probability remains above break-even. Otherwise, the segment is labeled \textit{Do Not Scale}. This rule prevents the business from investing in segments that appear behaviorally promising but fail to satisfy unit-economic constraints.

The same framework also supports break-even sensitivity analysis. For each segment, the required conversion rate is recalculated across a grid of gross margin assumptions (20\%, 30\%, 40\%, and 50\%) and CAC levels (\$5, \$10, \$15, and \$20). This pivot analysis identifies the economic conditions under which a campaign becomes viable and helps managers understand whether profitability depends more strongly on margin improvement, CAC reduction, or conversion uplift.


This optimization layer ensures that campaign recommendations are not based solely on descriptive segmentation or predictive scores. Instead, each segment is evaluated through a combined framework of purchase probability, expected AOV, gross margin, stochastic CAC, break-even conversion rate, and ROMI. The resulting decision rule provides a financial screening mechanism that helps prioritize segments with both behavioral potential and positive expected economic return.

\subsubsection{Predictive Segment Campaign Strategy}

The predictive segmentation phase partitions the active user 
base of 119,207 sessions into two clusters via K-Means, 
yielding a High-Value \text{Stars} segment (2,462 users, 
avg $p\_buy$ = 0.040) and a \text{Low Priority} segment 
(116,745 users, avg $p\_buy$ = 0.001), as summarized in 
Table~\ref{tab:kmeans_profile}. Given this disparity, campaign 
resources are distributed selectively rather than uniformly 
across the user base. The Stars segment is therefore 
designated as the primary campaign target, with the objective 
framed as conversion acceleration -- encouraging 
high-intent users to complete purchases through personalized 
engagement. The treatment design adopts a multi-layered 
approach aimed at reducing conversion friction and 
strengthening purchase intent:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Personalized Re-engagement Campaigns:
    Behavioral-triggered email or push notification campaigns
    leveraging recency, browsing depth, and product affinity
    signals to deliver contextually relevant reminders and
    offers.

    \item Exclusive Incentive Offers:
    Time-sensitive promotional incentives such as discounts,
    free-shipping offers, or loyalty rewards designed to
    reduce hesitation among high-intent users.

    \item Retargeting and Recommendation Strategies:
    Personalized recommendation systems and remarketing
    touchpoints aimed at re-engaging users who demonstrated
    strong browsing or cart activity but did not finalize
    checkout.
\end{itemize}

In contrast, the \text{Low Priority} segment is approached 
through low-cost awareness-oriented strategies. Given its 
substantially weaker purchase likelihood, high hibernation 
rate, and lower expected revenue contribution, aggressive 
conversion-focused spending on this group is unlikely to 
generate positive returns. The framework therefore prioritizes 
organic content, broad SEO, and seasonal touchpoints for this 
population, reserving performance marketing budget exclusively 
for the Stars segment.

The primary performance metric is the \text{Conversion Rate 
(CR)}, defined as the proportion of targeted users who 
complete a purchase following campaign exposure:

\begin{equation}
CR = \frac{\text{Number of purchasers post-exposure}}
          {\text{Total users targeted}}
\end{equation}

Campaign viability is evaluated by modeling expected 
profitability as:
\begin{equation}
\textit{Expected Profit} = \textit{Total\_Rev} \times CR 
\times GM - N \times CAC
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\textit{Total\_Rev}$ denotes the aggregate 
predicted revenue of the targeted segment (\$5,964 for 
Stars), $GM$ represents gross margin, $N$ is the number of 
targeted users (2,462), and $CAC$ is the cost per targeted 
user. Campaign efficiency is further quantified through the 
Return on Investment metric:

\begin{equation}
ROI = \frac{\textit{Expected Profit}}{N \times CAC}
\end{equation}

To assess robustness under varying business assumptions, 
the framework incorporates a sensitivity analysis across 
CAC, gross margin, and conversion rate scenarios. The 
minimum conversion threshold required for campaign 
profitability is defined as:

\begin{equation}
CR_{\text{break-even}} = \frac{N \times CAC}
{\textit{Total\_Rev} \times GM}
\end{equation}

Finally, campaign effectiveness is validated through a 
randomized A/B testing framework using the Stars segment 
as the primary experimental population. The detailed 
experimental protocol -- including treatment-control 
design, sample size estimation, statistical significance 
thresholds, and temporal controls for mitigating 
day-of-week effects -- is established in 
Section~\ref{sec:funnel_strategy_highest_dropoff}. The 
objective is to estimate the incremental conversion uplift 
generated by the campaign while ensuring both statistical 
validity and financial feasibility prior to full-scale 
deployment.

High-Value Engaged Buyers should be retained and rewarded, At-Risk Low-Activity Buyers should be reactivated, and Recent Quality Buyers should be nurtured toward repeat purchasing. This provides a practical foundation for lifecycle marketing and enables the CMO to allocate resources according to customer value, risk level, and future growth potential.

\subsection{Strategy and Experimental Design}
\subsubsection{Funnel Strategy for Highest Drop-off Point}

The funnel-based campaign is designed to address the most critical conversion gap in the customer journey: the transition from \textit{Product View} to \textit{Add to Cart}. This stage is selected because it represents the point where users have already shown product-level interest, but many of them still fail to express stronger purchase intent by adding the product to cart. Therefore, the campaign does not focus on broad traffic acquisition or final checkout optimization. Instead, it targets a narrower and more actionable behavioral problem: converting product viewers into cart adders.

The baseline funnel shows that there are 244,965 product view sessions and 79,263 sessions with both product view and add-to-cart behavior. This gives a baseline Product View to Add-to-Cart rate of 32.36\%. In addition, the downstream Add-to-Cart to Purchase rate is approximately 18.76\%, and the average order value is around \$127.17. These figures provide the foundation for evaluating whether an improvement in the Add-to-Cart rate can generate meaningful downstream business value.

\subsubsection{Segment-Level Campaign Optimization}

After the funnel campaign identifies which website experience should be improved, the segment-level campaign optimization determines where marketing budget should be allocated across historical customer segments. The purpose is not only to identify behaviorally attractive groups, but also to test whether each group can generate positive expected profit after accounting for average order value (AOV), gross margin (GM), customer acquisition cost (CAC), assumed conversion rate (CR), break-even CR, and ROMI. Since the revenue field is detected in micro format, all revenue values are first converted into standard currency units. The resulting buyer benchmark AOV is \$114.44, which serves as the reference point for assigning expected AOV assumptions across both buyer and non-buyer segments.

The economic logic is based on a simple unit-profit rule. Expected campaign profit increases when the expected gross contribution, measured by $N \times CR \times AOV \times GM$, is larger than the campaign acquisition cost, measured by $N \times CAC$. ROMI is then used as the scaling criterion, while the break-even CR shows the minimum conversion rate required for a segment to avoid negative profit under a given CAC and margin scenario.

Table~\ref{tab:segment_optimization_output} summarizes the final optimization output. The result is highly selective. Only \textit{High-Value Engaged Buyers} is recommended to \textit{Scale Aggressively}. This segment contains 5,020 customers, has the highest expected AOV at \$179.34, an assumed conversion rate of 24\%, and a positive ROMI of 1.26. Its simulated expected profit is \$54,161.28. All other segments are classified as \textit{Do Not Scale} because their assumed conversion rates are below the break-even requirement or because their expected contribution does not cover CAC after margin assumptions are applied.

The practical strategy should therefore be tiered. Margin-protective incentives such as free shipping thresholds, bundle savings, or member-only access are more appropriate than broad price cuts.

Second, \textit{Recent Quality Buyers} should not be scaled with expensive paid media despite their 18\% assumed CR, because their ROMI remains negative. A more realistic strategy is low-cost lifecycle automation, including post-purchase email sequences, second-purchase reminders, product replenishment prompts, and complementary product recommendations. \subsection{Final Comparison Segmentation}

The final comparison segmentation links the historical customer segmentation results with the predictive CLV-based segmentation. The purpose of this step is to evaluate whether customers who were historically classified as valuable or high-potential are also identified by the predictive model as future high-value customers. In other words, this comparison connects past behavioral evidence with forward-looking customer value prediction.

The historical segmentation dataset contains 1,272,948 users, while the prediction dataset contains 119,207 users. After merging the two datasets by \textit{fullVisitorId}, only 1,751 users appear in both datasets. This overlap group is important because it allows a direct comparison between historical segment labels and predictive segment labels for the same customers. Therefore, the analysis does not compare the two full datasets directly, but focuses on the matched users for whom both historical and predictive information are available.

The predictive model classifies only 79 out of the 1,751 matched customers as \textit{Stars}, equivalent to 4.51\% of the overlap sample. The remaining 1,672 users, or 95.49\%, are assigned to the \textit{Low Priority} group. This result confirms that the predicted high-value segment is highly selective. Rather than broadly labeling many customers as valuable, the model identifies a relatively small group with stronger future commercial potential. This is consistent with the CLV analysis, where the Stars segment represents a small but strategically important customer group.

A key finding is that all 79 predicted Stars come from historically meaningful or high-potential groups. Specifically, the Stars segment is composed of 31 Warm Prospects, 22 High-Value Engaged Buyers, 17 Hot Prospects, 7 Dormant Engaged Non-Buyers, and 2 Recent Quality Buyers. No predicted Stars come from General Non-Buyers, New Low-Intent Visitors, or At-Risk Low-Activity Buyers. This pattern suggests that the predictive model is behaviorally reasonable: it does not randomly assign future value to historically weak or low-intent users, but instead concentrates predicted value among users who previously showed either purchase value or strong engagement signals.

Among the historical groups, Warm Prospects contribute the largest number of predicted Stars, with 31 customers. This is strategically important because Warm Prospects were previously interpreted as interested non-buyers who had not yet converted. Their presence in the Stars segment suggests that some non-buyers may have substantial future value even if they have not generated revenue historically. Therefore, the company should not rely only on past purchase behavior when deciding campaign priorities. Users with strong engagement signals may also deserve targeted nurturing because they can become high-value customers in the future.

Overall, the final comparison segmentation provides a practical bridge between descriptive segmentation and predictive customer value modeling. Historical segmentation explains where customers came from and how they behaved in the past, while predictive segmentation estimates which customers are most likely to generate future value. The most important action group is therefore the intersection between historically valuable or high-potential customers and predicted Stars. In this analysis, that group contains 79 customers, representing 100\% of all predicted Stars but only 5.38\% of the historically good segments. This means that many customers may look promising based on historical behavior, but only a small subset is strong enough to justify priority investment under the predictive CLV framework.

From a strategic perspective, the company should use this comparison to move from broad segment-based marketing to precision targeting. High-Value Engaged Buyers and Hot Prospects should receive priority conversion and retention actions because they are supported by both historical behavior and predictive value. Warm Prospects should be treated as an important growth segment because they represent the largest source of predicted Stars. Dormant Engaged Non-Buyers and Recent Quality Buyers should be handled selectively, focusing only on those with high predicted value. Finally, General Non-Buyers, New Low-Intent Visitors, and most Low Priority users should be managed through low-cost nurturing or acquisition-quality improvement rather than expensive personalized campaigns.



\section{Conclusion}

This study proposed an end-to-end data-driven marketing
framework using the Google Merchandise Store dataset,
integrating behavioral preprocessing, customer segmentation,
predictive revenue modeling, and campaign strategy into a
unified analytical pipeline.

The preprocessing stage transformed raw clickstream logs
into structured behavioral features capturing RFM signals,
funnel progression, engagement quality, and temporal
patterns. Exploratory analysis highlighted several important
characteristics of the dataset, including severe transaction
imbalance, strong revenue concentration among a small subset
of customers, and weak long-term retention behavior.

Historical segmentation successfully identified distinct
buyer and non-buyer behavioral cohorts, enabling the
development of differentiated marketing strategies for
retention, reactivation, nurturing, and conversion
optimization. The predictive modeling stage further extended
this framework through a two-stage XGBoost hurdle
architecture designed to address the highly zero-inflated
revenue distribution. The resulting predictive scores were
used to construct actionable customer segments for targeted
campaign allocation.

Building upon these analytical outputs, the strategy
framework combined funnel optimization, predictive targeting,
and experimental validation into a unified decision-making
approach. Funnel interventions focused on reducing
View-to-Add-to-Cart friction, while predictive campaign
allocation concentrated marketing resources on
high-propensity users identified by the CLV model.
Sensitivity analysis and A/B testing design were further
incorporated to evaluate both statistical feasibility and
economic viability prior to deployment.

Several limitations should be acknowledged. The analysis is
restricted to a single retailer dataset and historical time
window, potentially limiting generalizability across broader
e-commerce contexts. In addition, the extremely imbalanced
purchase distribution constrains the amount of positive
training data available for revenue prediction. Campaign
evaluation also relies on assumed CAC and margin scenarios
rather than live experimental observations.

Future work may extend this framework through sequential or
deep learning architectures capable of capturing richer
temporal browsing dynamics. Additional opportunities include
the deployment of live experimentation infrastructure for
real-time causal validation, as well as dynamic cohort
tracking systems that continuously update customer value
states over time.
